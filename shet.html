Small ponies have existed in the Shetland Isles for over 2000 years.
The Shetland Pony is thought to be developed from a cob-type pony from Southern Europe and a pony brought to the islands by the Celtic people.
Numerous children used Shetland ponies for riding and driving.
For their size, they are the strongest of all the horse breeds, and when the law in 1847 banned children from entering the coal pits, the Shetland pony colts became in great demand.
Queen Victoria even owned several pairs of Shetland for driving her smart phaetons (light, open, four-wheel, horse-drawn carriages).
The Shetland Pony Stud-Book Society was formed in 1890, with the aim of publishing a Stud-Book (a book containing the pedigrees of horses).
These ponies are about 107 cm high at four years old or over.
Their coat changes according to the season, which helps protect them against the often harsh conditions of the islands.
The Independent wrote an article in 2016 stating that,"There are 50 ponies to every one human being on Foula Island [part of the Shetland islands]".
